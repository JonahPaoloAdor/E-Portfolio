Challenging Firm Independence: Enhancing Bankruptcy Prediction with Industry-Adjusted Cox Proportional Hazards Models

Undergraduate Thesis  ·  De La Salle University  ·  2024–2025

This study challenges a core assumption of one of the most widely used bankruptcy prediction tools in finance — the Cox Proportional Hazards Model (CPHM) — which treats all firms as failing independently of one another. In reality, firms within the same industry share common risk exposures, regulatory environments, and market shocks, making their financial distress events correlated, not independent.

Using data from publicly listed U.S. firms from 1999 to 2019, the industry-adjusted model materially outperformed the standard approach:

0.630 Standard Model C-index
0.708 Industry-Adjusted Model C-index

This finding has direct relevance to audit risk assessment, going-concern evaluations, and financial advisory — areas where accurate distress prediction is essential to protecting investors, creditors, and the public interest.

Philosophy

Personal Values & Professional Philosophy

I entered these professions believing that technical excellence is necessary but not sufficient. A capable professional must also be principled — one who sees the human dimension behind every financial statement and every audit finding.

01

Integrity & Independence

Audit exists because markets need trust. I take seriously the auditor's obligation to independence — not merely as a regulatory requirement, but as a moral commitment. When I evaluate evidence, I do so with the intent to report what is true, not what is convenient. This commitment to honest judgment, even when difficult, is the foundation on which I intend to build my professional reputation.

02

Analytical Courage

My thesis challenged a dominant assumption in bankruptcy prediction methodology — not out of contrarianism, but because the evidence warranted a different conclusion. Rigorous thinking requires the willingness to question established frameworks and propose alternatives grounded in data. This is the same disposition I bring to financial analysis and audit practice: to look beyond surface-level compliance and ask whether the underlying economic reality is accurately represented.

03

Service & the Common Good

As a Lasallian, I was formed in the tradition of service — particularly to those who are most vulnerable. In my career, this translates into work that protects the public interest: audits that prevent fraud, financial models that support sound decisions, and advisory work that creates sustainable value. I am aware that the financial profession carries significant responsibility, and I do not take that lightly.
